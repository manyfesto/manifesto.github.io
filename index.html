AI Manyfesto

This manyfesto is a provocation, a question, an opening, a dance about a future of AI technologies that is decolonial. We call it manyfesto, since it reflects some truths among many, and we hope to invite exchange, conversation, and the development of more such statements from people affected by AI technology.

We begin with the challenge posed by the language we use to talk about AI: language that has emerged, as much of the technology has, dominated by Western voices, whiteness, and wealth. We seek to uncover, to question, upend, and reinvent the assumptions underlying this language, even as we use it.

“Artificial” and “intelligence” are loaded terms, their definitions subject to cultural biases. AI is a technology, a science, a business, a knowledge system, a set of narratives, of relationships, an imaginary. Across each facet, our effort is to undo the colonial erasure of non-Western ways of being and knowing. The word “decoloniality,” too, resonates differently in different communities, including with Indigenous peoples and those for whom colonialism is not a history but a present reality. Some reject the term decolonial in this context. We validate both its use and its rejection.

We do not seek consensus: we value human difference. We reject the idea that any one framework could govern globally. We reject the Western-normative language of “ethical” AI and suggestions of “inclusivity” that do not destabilize current patterns of domination. We reject as half-measures any principles meant to tweak, reinforce, and whitewash the status quo, merely blunting its devastation. They fail to acknowledge how, in present systems, the social and the technical are interwoven, and technologies have immaterial as well as material impacts over specific gendered, racialized bodies and territories. Decoloniality rejects the divorcing of the material and immaterial, of feeling from being, knowing, doing or living.

Notions of decolonial governances will emerge from community and context, questioning what currently constitutes legitimate authority. Decolonialilty is not merely diversity and inclusion; removing the echoes of coloniality in AI will require reparations for past economic, epistemic, and structural injustice and dispossession. These reinventions of AI governance will acknowledge the expertise that comes from lived experience, and create new pathways to make it possible for those who have historically been marginalized to have the opportunity to decide and build their own dignified socio-technical futures. Decolonial governance will recognize, in a way that Western-centric governance structures historically have not, how our roles and destinies are intertwined.

Our humanity is reflexive, defined by how we relate to one another, including through technology. We owe each other our mutual futures. The primordial vectors of art and narrative are among the paths we will explore. We seek to center the engineering, design, knowledge-production, and dispute-resolution practices of diverse cultures, which are embedded with their own divergent value systems.

Our urgency arises from humans’ capacity to use AI as a knowledge system to create irrefutable “algorithmic truths” to reinforce domination. In doing so, other systems of knowledge production and other truths are denied and erased, as are other peoples’ agency, autonomy, and contestation. In this way AI colonialism extends beyond data colonialism: not merely extractive, the use of AI has the capacity to shape reality. Designed in an unequal society, these systems can be employed to reproduce those inequalities. Built with an emphasis on speed rather than safety, they can do irreparable harm. In insisting on a decolonial AI, we stand for the right of each community to reshape that reality on their terms.

Our methods will evolve, sensitive to needs and opportunities, but our aim is to create and hold a resonant forum for learning and exchange from and between voices silenced by colonialist structures.
